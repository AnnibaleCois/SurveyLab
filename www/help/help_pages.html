The SurveyLab world

The SurveyLab world consist of 110 Towns distributed over 5 Regions, for a total population of about 250000 people. Each town has a Municipal Office and one or more Health Facilities (Clinics and Hospitals). The main Town in each region, and a few other, have also an Environmental Health Office. Town are subdivided in Suburbs.

Aggregated information about the SurveyLab world and its population can be accessed by 'visiting' Municipal Offices, Health Facilities and Environmental Health Offices. They are indicated in the maps with these symbols:

Health Facilities

Visit health facilities to get information about services offered, staff-complement, health statistics,...

Environmental Health Offices

Visit environmental health offices to get information about the environment: altitude, termal excursion, air pollution levels,...

Building a data collection tool

You can design you data collection tool for the survey by dragging the relevant questions/measurements from the lists on the right and dropping them on the left section (Current tool).

The RESET button empties the Current tool section and resets the lists of available items to the default status.

Defining a sampling strategy

To define the sampling strategy, you must first identify the target population and choose (1) the geographic scope (either the whole SurveyLab or specific Regions), (2) the desired age range, and (3) the sex of the potential respondents.


The Sampling stages section requires information regarding stratification (none, by Region, by Town, by Suburb), clustering (none, one level, two levels) and sample sizes at the various levels.

Depending on the choices, different type of information need to be provided. The most complex case is when the chosen strategy is two-level stratified random sampling, where information needs to be provided on stratification, clustering variables and sample size at each level, type of cluster selection (proportional to size or not).

If the you don't make any selection, the system uses a default sampling strategy with these characteristics (simple random sampling of size 100):

  • Target population: whole surveylab, both sexes, age range 15+
  • Stratification: None
  • Clusters: None
  • Sample size: 100

Note: it is your responsibility to ensure that the sampling parameters are meaningful. The strategy page will accept any set of parameters. Only at the sampling stage, when the actual selection of the respondents takes place, an incongruent request (i.e. requesting to select a number of clusters greater than the number of clusters in the stratum) will cause the impossibility of carrying out the procedure as requested.

Selecting households and Individuals

Two types of sample selection are possible:

  • Systematic (manual) sampling
  • Random sampling

With the systematic sampling strategy, you conduct a manual selection of the individuals to be included in the sample. The procedure includes (1) Selection of towns, (2) Selection of households within a town and (3) election of individuals within a household.

With the random sampling strategy, the system will randomly sample the population according to the strategy defined in the Strategy page.


In both cases, the results of the sampling is visualised by pressing the OVERVIEW button. If the system identifies some incongruence in the sampling strategy (such as the request of selecting in a stratum more individuals than those available in the population), you'll be notified. Power analysis

In this Power analysis page, you can conduct a preliminary analysis of the precision that can be obtained with the chosen sampling strategy. You need to select outcome for which the results need to be calculated, and the basic parameters upon which the calculation will be based (expected proportion/standard deviation, design effect and minimum response rate). Alternatively, the system can calculate the parameters from the underlying population, when the you checks the “Auto” checkbox.

The Precision and cost panel will show the results of the calculation, which include an estimate of the total survey cost and unit cost. The system estimates administration times and costs using an algorithm that attributes different weights to different types of items in the data collection tool (e.g. it attributes longer administration times and costs to anthropometric measurements compared to questions). The calculations take into account increases in setup costs, when a survey spans across multiple towns, and the costs within each town based on the total distance a fieldworker has to walk/drive to visit each selected household.

Conducting a survey

When a valid sample has been selected and a data collection tool has been defined in the respective pages, you can start a (virtual) data collection. The survey can be conducted town by town, by dragging the data collector to the respective town:

or for all towns at the same time, by pressing the SURVEY button.

Check your data

This Analysis page summarises the results of the survey and allows for comaprions with the 'true' population values. You can find:

  • A summary of the sample and data collection tool
  • A comparison of the estimated survey cost and the actaul cost (whicht takes into account the response rates)
  • A graphical/tabular summary of the response rates (total, by region and by town)

The comparions of the survey results with the 'true' population values is done variable by variable, and can refer tp the whole population or by subgroup.
